Motivation & Research

This section explains why we chose the XJTLU Campus Tour project and how academic research and existing products informed our design direction.

The Why

Why this project matters

We chose this project because traditional campus visits often rely on guided tours and one-way lectures, which make visitors passive listeners rather than active explorers. At XJTLU, this problem is especially significant because the university’s student-centered philosophy cannot be fully understood through explanations alone. It is better experienced through direct interaction with classrooms, learning spaces, and campus culture. For new students, traditional tours may provide information but often fail to create a memorable sense of connection with the university. For external visitors, especially educators and administrators joining ILEAD activities, such visits may present the campus physically but do not always communicate the deeper educational ideas behind each space.

Our project aims to transform the campus tour into a mobile, interactive, and playful experience. By combining location-based guidance, gamified check-ins, XR elements, and AI-supported interaction, the system encourages users to explore the campus independently and engage with each site more deeply. This helps visitors move beyond simply seeing buildings to understanding how XJTLU designs learning environments to support innovation, collaboration, and student autonomy.

The Gap

What existing work does well and where it falls short

To identify opportunities for improvement, we reviewed both academic papers and commercial products related to location-based AR, campus orientation, mobile navigation, and playful exploration. The papers helped us understand educational value, technical feasibility, and design challenges, while the products showed practical patterns for route guidance, storytelling, and engagement.

By comparing their strengths and limitations, we identified which ideas could be adapted for XJTLU’s campus context and which issues should be avoided in our own design, especially in terms of usability, role-based content, and balancing learning with interaction.

Academic Research

4 papers reviewed to understand educational value, feasibility, and design risks.

Paper 01

Location-Based Augmented Reality in Education

Kleftodimos & Evagelou (2025)

Relevance: Explains how location-based AR connects learning content with real-world places.

Strengths

  • • Clearly explains the educational value of location-based AR.
  • • Describes triggering technologies such as GPS and indoor positioning.
  • • Provides practical examples from heritage and outdoor learning.

Limitations

  • • Lacks strong empirical evidence.
  • • Focuses more on technology than UX challenges.
  • • Many examples are closer to heritage learning than campus navigation.

Paper 02

Using a Location-Based AR Mobile App to Support New Students’ Orientation

Taşyonar & Uzun (2025)

Relevance: Directly studies AR-supported campus orientation for new students.

Strengths

  • • Highly relevant to campus orientation.
  • • Clearly explains implementation and system design.
  • • Includes real student testing and empirical evidence.

Limitations

  • • Did not outperform traditional guided orientation in knowledge learning.
  • • Reported technical and device compatibility issues.
  • • Small sample size limits generalisability.

Paper 03

A Game-based AR Navigation System for Makerspace Education

Chen, Hwang & Lin (2023)

Relevance: Shows how AR and gamification can support active exploration.

Strengths

  • • Combines AR with gamified missions and rewards.
  • • Provides a clear and structured interaction flow.
  • • Supports active learning through problem-solving tasks.

Limitations

  • • May prioritise engagement over learning depth.
  • • Context is limited to a library makerspace.
  • • Interaction design may be too complex for general mobile users.

Paper 04

Location-based AR in Education: A Systematic Literature Review

Fonseca et al. (2025)

Relevance: Provides a broad overview of location-based AR in educational settings.

Strengths

  • • Reviews a wide range of educational use cases.
  • • Demonstrates strong potential for engagement and experiential learning.
  • • Identifies common trends such as field trips and outdoor learning.

Limitations

  • • Many studies still lack strong experimental evidence.
  • • Limited integration into formal education.
  • • Technical and usability challenges remain significant.

Commercial Benchmarks

4 products reviewed to analyse navigation, storytelling, exploration, and interaction patterns.

Product 01

Google Maps

Why relevant: A strong reference for map-based navigation and place information.

Strengths

  • • Strong navigation and map support.
  • • Familiar interface with low learning cost.
  • • Integrates place-related information effectively.

Limitations

  • • Designed for navigation rather than guided learning.
  • • Does not provide AR narration.
  • • Does not distinguish between different user roles.

Product 02

izi.TRAVEL

Why relevant: Useful for self-paced exploration and location-based storytelling.

Strengths

  • • Supports location-based audio guidance.
  • • Encourages self-paced exploration.
  • • Combines route structure with storytelling.

Limitations

  • • More audio-focused than AR-focused.
  • • Has limited playful interaction.
  • • Weak support for differentiated user roles.

Product 03

Pokémon GO

Why relevant: Demonstrates how location-linked interaction can motivate exploration.

Strengths

  • • Demonstrates strong location-based interaction.
  • • Creates a strong sense of exploration and engagement.
  • • Blends AR-style interaction with everyday movement.

Limitations

  • • Designed for entertainment rather than education.
  • • Lacks structured explanatory content.
  • • Does not support user-specific information delivery.

Product 04

Actionbound

Why relevant: A useful model for mission-based exploration and active participation.

Strengths

  • • Supports task-based and route-based exploration.
  • • Encourages active participation.
  • • Flexible for educational content design.

Limitations

  • • Has limited AR emphasis.
  • • Depends heavily on creator setup quality.
  • • May feel more like a scavenger hunt than a guided AR tour.
Key Design Insights

What we learned from research

Our review suggests that an effective campus tour should combine clear navigation, self-paced exploration, meaningful storytelling, and light gamification. However, it should avoid overly complex tasks, purely entertainment-driven mechanics, and one-size-fits-all information delivery. Based on these findings, our project aims to create a mobile experience that is educational, location-aware, and engaging, while also adapting content for different user groups such as new students and external visitors.

Key Concepts

📍

Location-Based Interaction

The application detects the user's geographic position and triggers interactive XR content when the user approaches predefined campus landmarks.

🤖

AR Guide

A virtual guide character appears to introduce campus locations through narration and AI-supported interaction.

✨

Immersive Exploration

Transforming a simple walk into an engaging story-driven tour supported by check-ins, digital rewards, and student-centered interpretation.

Design Process

01

Initial Concept

The project began with the idea of improving the campus tour experience for visitors and new students. Traditional tours often rely on static maps or human guides, which may not always be available.

02

Research

Research focused on extended reality technologies, location-based interaction systems, existing digital tour applications, and the educational goals of ILEAD visits.

03

Interaction Design

Different interaction models were explored, including route guidance, AR trigger logic, gamified check-ins, and role-specific content for visitors and students.

04

Prototype Development

A prototype was developed to demonstrate mobile navigation, location-triggered storytelling, digital guidance, and a more engaging campus orientation flow.

05

Testing and Refinement

The prototype was refined by improving the information hierarchy, simplifying tasks, clarifying navigation cues, and making the mobile experience more intuitive.

Stakeholder Insights

Core stakeholder groups and user needs behind the project direction.

New Students

New students need orientation support, clearer spatial understanding, and a more memorable introduction to XJTLU’s learning environment. They benefit from accessible mobile guidance, self-paced exploration, and lightweight interaction.

External Visitors

External visitors, especially teachers and administrators joining ILEAD activities, need a deeper understanding of XJTLU’s student-centered philosophy. They require interpretive content rather than simple building introductions.

Pain Points of Traditional Visits

  • • Guided tours and lectures make visitors passive receivers of information.
  • • Important educational ideas are difficult to remember after a single visit.
  • • Traditional tours show physical spaces but not always the deeper meaning behind them.
  • • Different user groups need different content emphasis, but current visits are usually one-size-fits-all.

Reflection

What we learned

Through this project, we learned that designing a meaningful campus tour is not only about building navigation features, but also about translating educational ideas into interactive experiences. Our research showed that engagement increases when users actively explore, but the system must remain simple enough for mobile use.

Future improvement

In the next stage, we hope to strengthen live usability testing, improve information differentiation for visitors and students, and further integrate AI-supported interactions with place-based storytelling. We also need to carefully consider accessibility, device compatibility, and the ethical use of AI-generated assets.
